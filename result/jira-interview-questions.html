2) Explain Jira workflow.

In Jira, a workflow is a series of actions taken to manage an issue throughout its lifecycle. These actions are called transitions, and they reflect the movement of an issue from one status to another. Each status signifies the impact of the work done on the issue, and the overall flow of the workflow is designed to help testers efficiently coordinate and track their work.

The Jira workflow is widely referred to as the "defect lifecycle" or the "bug lifecycle." This lifecycle comprises several phases, including:

  • Open issue

  • In Progress
    This is the phase where the software developers start working on the issue.
  • Resolved issue
    When the issue is resolved by the software developer and waiting for the verification by the software tester. If the verification is successful, then the issue is closed; otherwise, the issue gets reopened.
  • Reopened issue
    If the verification is unsuccessful, then the issue is reopened and assigned to the software developer.
  • Close issue
    If the verification of the fixed bug is successful, then the issue is closed by the tester.

12) Explain Kanban board?

A Kanban board is a useful tool for displaying and managing tasks while also controlling the amount of work in progress.

In Scrum and Kanban, tasks are taken from a product backlog and prioritized. While in Scrum, tasks are added to a Sprint Backlog and completed within a designated time frame, Kanban doesn't have a sprint and tasks are completed on an ongoing basis. Therefore, the main difference between Scrum and Kanban is that Scrum includes a Sprint Backlog while Kanban doesn't.

A Kanban board typically includes three stages:

  • To Do
  • Doing
  • Done

At the beginning of a project, all the tasks in the product backlog are added to the 'To Do' section. As team members begin working on a task, it is moved to the 'Doing' section, and once it is completed, it is moved to the 'Done' section.

By using a Kanban board, it's possible to track completed tasks and identify which ones still need attention and development.

A crucial aspect of the Kanban board is its ability to set limits. In the depicted scenario, there are eight tasks in the product backlog and a maximum limit of four. This means that only four tasks can be in the 'To Do' state at any given time. In the event that one of these tasks is moved to the 'Doing' state, another task from the product backlog can be moved to the 'To Do' state. The limit can be adjusted based on the availability of resources.

20) What are the differences between Scrum board and Kanban board?

Here are the distinctions between a Scrum board and a Kanban board:

Scrum Kanban
Planning Agile methodology involves a structured approach to planning and execution. The process begins with sprint planning and concludes with a sprint review and retrospective. Throughout the process, the team engages in daily meetings to stay informed about upcoming tasks, priorities, and lessons learned from previous tasks. The Kanban methodology is characterized by a lack of predetermined planning and the absence of daily meetings. Instead, it allows for changes to be made at any time, resulting in a high frequency of alterations.
Timeline Scrum methodology involves working on a time-bound sprint, where a deliverable is provided to the client within a fixed duration. In Kanban, there is no concept of a sprint, and as such, there is no predetermined timeline for product delivery to the client.

They carefully select the number of items so that they can be realistically completed within the sprint duration, usually 1-4 weeks.
