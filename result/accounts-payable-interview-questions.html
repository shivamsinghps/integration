Most Asked Accounts Payable Interview Questions

Below are some commonly asked questions during an interview for an Accounts Payable position, along with some suggested answers.

1) What do you understand by Account Payable? Explain it with an example.

Accounts payable refers to the amount that a business owes to vendors for goods and services purchased on credit. This represents an existing liability that must be paid by the company to its creditors or suppliers within a specified timeframe.

Accounts payable refers to the amount owed by a company to its suppliers or vendors for goods and services received but not yet paid for. It is a current liability and is considered a short-term debt that needs to be paid to avoid default. In other words, it is the money that a company owes to its vendors or suppliers for their products or services.

Let's consider a scenario where a restaurant buys food and beverages from a company on credit basis. In this case, the restaurant owes money to the company which falls under the category of trade payables. Meanwhile, there may be other obligations that the restaurant has to fulfill with various companies such as cleaning uniforms for staff, which are classified as accounts payable. Although both of these categories come under the broader term of accounts payable, some companies may simply refer to them using this single term.

2) What is the meaning of TDS? How is it charged?

TDS is an initialism used to denote Tax Deducted at Source, which is applied on the Base Amount. It is a straightforward process of deducting tax. Whenever a party (also known as the deductor) is obligated to pay another party (also referred to as the deductee), the deductor will subtract the TDS and transmit the balance payment to the deductee. The TDS sum is subsequently sent to the Central Government.

There exist two primary forms of TDS:

  • Here's a rephrased version: TDS, or Tax Deducted at Source, is a system introduced by the government of India to collect taxes from different sources of income. It applies to salaried employees, wherein a portion of their salary is deducted as income tax before they receive their pay. This system helps ensure that individuals pay their taxes on time and also helps streamline the tax collection process for the government. TDS on salaried employees must be deducted from the salary paid to the employer if it crosses the tax limit. This money has to be paid to the government on or before the due date.
  • The topic is about TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) on contractors and other professionals. Here's a rephrased version: The focus of this discussion centers around the concept of TDS or Tax Deducted at Source and its application to contractors and various professionals. In TDS on contractors or other professionals, there is a slab that min tax, surcharge, education cess plus higher education cess. The finance minister regulates it, and they can make changes or keep the same or remove in every year budget. You can deduct the TDS on the bill amount while paying or if an advance is paid.

4) What is the difference between debenture holder and preference shareholder?

The following outlines notable distinctions between preference shareholders and debenture holders.

Debenture Holder Preference Shareholder
A debenture holder is an individual who holds debentures, which represents an owed debt to a company. A preference shareholder refers to an individual who possesses shares in a company. Shareholders can be deemed as co-owners of the company.
The holder of a debenture is entitled to receive payment regardless of the financial performance of the company they have invested in. If an individual is a preference shareholder, they are entitled to receive dividend payments only if the company is profitable.
A holder of a debenture simply holds a debt position with respect to the company and is not considered an owner of the company. A shareholder who holds preference shares is considered as a co-owner of the company.
When someone invests in a debenture, they receive interest on their investment until the capital is repaid or until the end of the agreed term. A shareholder who holds preference shares does not have a guaranteed return on their investment. They receive dividends as long as the company remains profitable and in existence.
Debenture holders are not allowed to participate in the company's annual general meeting except if there is any matter impacting their stakeholder rights and interests. The company usually extends an invitation to the preference shareholders to attend the annual general meeting.
Debenture holders do not have the authority to meddle with the company's management and governance. The company's affairs are controlled by its shareholders, who elect representatives to the Board of Directors to manage its operations.
It is possible to exchange debentures for shares through a process known as conversion. It is not possible to change or exchange shares for debentures.
Debentures are typically backed by collateral and have a claim on the company's assets. This means that in the event of liquidation, the debenture holder's claims are prioritized over those of shareholders. Unlike some other financial instruments, shares don't come with a charge to return the capital invested. This means that in most cases, the money invested in shares cannot be returned to the investor, unless the shares in question are redeemable preference shares.

5) What do you understand by a Non-PO Invoice?

To put it simply, a Non-PO Invoice refers to an invoice that lacks a purchase order. In order to facilitate payment, such invoices require approval from those who are authorized to approve them.

9) What do you understand by liabilities?

Liability refers to an obligation that a person or company owes, typically in the form of a specific sum of money. These obligations are usually paid over a period of time by transferring economic benefits. In simpler terms, it is an ongoing duty between two parties that has not yet been fulfilled or paid off.

10) What do you understand by the term Debit Balance? How can we recover it if we won't have any future transactions from the supplier?

A debit balance is a sum of money that a customer must keep in their account to ensure that a purchase order is properly settled. When a transaction occurs, the debit balance serves as security. If the customer has a debit balance, we need to issue a credit memo to their regular vendors to recover the balance. If there are no future transactions with the supplier in question, we request that they send a check or make an EFT for the amount due.

One approach for addressing a debit balance is to reach out to the vendor and request that they return the excess funds or issue a refund. Alternatively, the surplus amount can be adjusted in future invoices from that vendor.

12) What do you understand by Open Item Management?

The practice of open item management ensures that documents in a system retain unprocessed items until they are resolved. Archiving is only possible once all open items in a document are cleared.

13) What do you understand by wire transfer?

Wire transfer is an electronic mode of funds transfer between two banking accounts using the SWIFT network. These digital payments are processed by adding the beneficiary account to the payer's account, which releases the funds immediately and credits the beneficiary account.

14) What do you understand by IFA?

The Institute of Financial Accountants (IFA) is an organization that represents and certifies financial accountants in the UK. The IFA is a member of the International Federation of Accountants and focuses on supporting small businesses. It was established in 1916 with the mission of making small businesses count.

15) What is STPI? What is the main objective of STPI?

STPI is an acronym for Software Technology Parks of India. It was founded by the Indian Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology in 1991 as a science and technology institution. Its primary goal is to facilitate the exportation of software from India, as well as to provide resources such as infrastructure, mentoring, and market connectivity to startups. It is also dedicated to skill development and support for entrepreneurs looking to establish their businesses in the tech industry.

16) Why is the knowledge of STPI required in Accounts Payable?

Familiarity with STPI is crucial in the field of Accounts Payable because certain software service providers receive exemptions from STPI for their software exports. As such, it is necessary to specify the vendor's STPI location in Accounts Payable based on their area of operations. Since there are various STPI locations in India, it is important to maintain the vendor's STPI according to their supply of products or services to one or multiple STPI locations.

17) What do you understand by Fringe Benefits Tax or FBT?

FBT is an abbreviation for Fringe Benefits Tax, which pertains to the tax levied on certain perks or advantages provided by employers to their employees, in addition to their regular salary or wages. One example of such a perk could be a novated lease.

The Finance Act 2005 introduced Fringe Benefit Tax (FBT) on April 1, 2006, which was calculated on 30% of the cost of benefits incurred by the company. FBT was an extra tax that was applied in addition to income tax, regardless of whether or not the company had an income tax obligation.

18) What are some examples of fringe benefits?

In addition to paying their employees a salary or compensation, many companies also offer various fringe benefits as part of their overall employment package. These benefits can include things like reimbursement for phone expenses, employer contributions to a superannuation fund, tuition assistance, health insurance, subsidized child care, discounted cafeteria, free transportation services, employee discounts, and stock options.

19) What do you understand by a three-way match?

The three-way match is a verification process that helps prevent incorrect payments. It involves matching the invoice, purchase order, and receipt report to ensure accurate purchase details before releasing payment. Its main objective is to minimize the risk of fraud and financial losses by avoiding payment for unauthorized purchases.

To ensure accuracy in financial transactions, it is necessary to use the three-way match process. This involves the careful comparison of three documents, such as a purchase order, receipt of goods, and invoice. This process helps identify any discrepancies and ensures that payments are made only for goods that have been received and approved. It is important to follow the proper procedures during the three-way match process to maintain financial integrity.

20) What is a trade discount? What is its benefit?

A trade discount refers to the discount or price reduction given by a manufacturer or wholesaler to a retailer or another wholesaler when purchasing products in bulk. This implies that a trade discount represents a particular percentage of the manufacturer's listed price that the manufacturer is willing to reduce, specifically for wholesalers or retailers. Discounts range between 20% to 40% based on the quantity of goods purchased.

The primary advantage of offering trade discounts is that it can incentivize customers to purchase products in larger quantities, effectively increasing sales. As such, the trade discount offered typically corresponds to the quantity of products bought.

21) What is the difference between Consignor and Consignee?

The term ‘consignor’ refers to the individual or entity that owns the merchandise being consigned and is accountable for providing it to the consignee. This can include goods producers, wholesalers, and other similar entities.

Consignee refers to the individual or entity that acquires and takes possession of goods. They are not the owners, but may include suppliers, distributors, or other parties involved in the distribution of goods.

22) What do you understand by an early payment discount?

An early payment discount is a form of discount offered to customers who pay their bills before the due date as specified in the purchase order. This type of discount is provided by suppliers in accordance with the terms and conditions of the order.

To illustrate, let's consider a scenario where ABC Company has sold goods worth one million rupees to XYZ Company with a payment term of 30 days from the invoice date. To encourage early payment, ABC has offered a 5% discount if XYZ Company makes the payment within one week from the invoice date. This is a typical example of an early payment discount.
