4) Write the difference between vector and raster graphics?

The subsequent points highlight the disparities between vector and raster graphics:

Raster or Bitmap images are limited by their resolution, which means that resizing them can result in a loss of image quality. Hence, it's crucial to consider the required size of the image before choosing a raster format.

The resolution of a vector-based display is not a limiting factor and can be resized without losing image quality. This is because vector images are not made up of pixels, but rather consisting of mathematical equations defining the lines, curves and shapes that make up the image. Therefore, the size of a vector image can be altered as needed while maintaining its clarity and sharpness.

Vector images are not suitable for creating realistic images, unlike raster images. This is because vector images are constructed using solid-color areas and mathematical gradients, which are unable to accurately recreate the continuous variation of shades and hues found in natural photographs.

6) Define Aspect Ratio?

Aspect ratio indicates the proportional relationship between the height and width of a screen, which enables vertical and horizontal lines of equal length to appear visually similar. For instance, an aspect ratio of 3:4 implies that a vertical line drawn with three units has the same length as a horizontal line drawn with four units.

7) Differentiate between Raster and Vector Graphics?

Raster Graphics Vector Graphics
Raster graphics are images comprised of small, individual pixels. Vector graphics are created using paths, rather than pixels, making them scalable without losing quality.
When a raster image is scaled up, its individual pixels do not contain information about how it should look when enlarged. Regardless of the scale at which it is viewed or printed, vector images maintain their visual qualities and do not lose their appearance.
Raster graphics cannot be resized without compromising their quality. Vector graphics can be resized without losing their quality.

9) Difference between DDA and Bresenham's line drawing algorithm.

Basics DDA Algorithm Bresenham's line Algorithms
Arithmetic The DDA algorithm employs Real Arithmetic that deals with floating point numbers. Bresenham's algorithm applies integer arithmetic or fixed-point computations to perform its calculations.
Operations The DDA algorithm utilizes multiplication and division when performing its operations. Bresenham's algorithm is a computational approach that involves utilizing addition and subtraction solely while performing operations.
Speed The DDA algorithm for line drawing is known to be slower compared to Bresenham's algorithm because it involves the use of real arithmetic, which relies on floating-point operations. Bresenham's algorithm is a quicker alternative to the DDA algorithm for inline drawing as it utilizes integer arithmetic and only involves addition and subtraction in its calculations, resulting in a significant speed improvement.
Accuracy & Efficiency The DDA algorithm is less precise and effective compared to Bresenham's algorithm. Bresenham's algorithm is known for its higher level of precision compared to DDA algorithms.
Drawing The DDA algorithm is capable of drawing circles and curves, but its level of accuracy is inferior to that of Bresenham's algorithm. Bresenham's algorithm is a method for drawing circles and curves that is more accurate than the DDA algorithm.
Expensive The DDA algorithm is expensive due to its dependence on a lot of calculations involving floating-point multiplications. According to the comparison between Bresenham's algorithm and DDA algorithm, it is found that Bresenham's algorithm is more cost-effective as it utilizes only addition and subtraction compared to the DDA algorithm.

14) Define Clipping and Clip window.

A clipping algorithm or clipping is a technique for identifying parts of a display that are within or outside a specific area of space. The area against which an object is clipped is called a clip window.

15) Differentiate between parallel projections from perspective projection.

Parallel Projection Perspective Projection
Parallel projection is a method of creating two-dimensional images of three-dimensional objects. It involves transforming the coordinates of the object to the view plane along lines that are parallel to each other. This technique is often used in engineering and architecture to create technical drawings and schematics. Perspective projection involves altering the positions of objects onto the view plane by using lines that meet at a particular point referred to as the projection reference point or center of projection.

16) What is the need for space partitioning representation?

Space partitioning representations are a valuable tool for creating interior algorithms. They involve dividing the spatial domain of an object into numerous small and adjacent solids that do not overlap. One commonly used form of space partitioning is the octree representation, which is especially popular for rendering 3D objects.

17) What is the quadric surfaces?

Quadric surfaces are a family of surfaces defined by second-degree equations known as quadrics. They comprise of various shapes such as ellipsoids, spheres, hyperboloids, paraboloids, and tori. In graphics, spheres and ellipsoids are commonly used as fundamental objects to construct more complex shapes.

18) What is critical fusion frequency?

The rate at which light waves are emitted or received, leading to the steady perception of a continuing sensation, is referred to as the critical frequency of light. This frequency can be influenced by a variety of factors, such as brightness, color, and contrast.

19) Difference between CMY and HSV color models.

CMY Model HSV Model
The CMY color model, which uses cyan, magenta, and yellow as primary colors, is a valuable tool for determining color output for printed materials. The HSV model is based on color descriptors that are more user-friendly and natural than other models. It uses hue (H), saturation (S), and value (V) as its primary color functions.
Color images can be produced on paper using hard-copy devices like plotters that apply color pigments onto the paper surface. To define a color, a person can choose a spectral hue and specify the proportions of black and white to be added in order to create various shades, tints, and tones.

20) What is dithering?

The term "dithering" has varied applications. It is commonly used to refer to methods used to create halftone patterns that appear continuous without reducing the resolution of an image, as such reduction can result in grid-like patterns. Additionally, the term can also be used to describe techniques that employ pixel grids to mimic halftones, or even be exclusively applied to color halftone approximation methods.

Dither noise is the term used for the addition of random values to pixel intensities, which helps to break up contours and create a more natural-looking image.

Computer Animation refers to the process of displaying visual changes in a scene over time. This can involve transformations such as movements or rotations as well as alterations in object properties like color, texture, or transparency. 23) Define Keyframe systems.

Key-frame systems refer to specialized animation languages that enable the creation of in-between frames based on user-provided keyframes. In these systems, each object in a given scene is defined as a group of rigid bodies connected at the joints, and with restricted degrees of freedom. In-between frames are then generated from the specification of two or more keyframes. Motion paths can be described kinematically using a set of spline curves, or physically based by outlining the force applied to the object being animated.

24) What is Fractals?

Fractals are unique geometric shapes that possess a distinctive property of roughness, which remains constant even if the shape is magnified to any degree. These shapes are self-similar, appearing identical at every level of scale.

28) What is the Koch curve?

To draw the Koch curve, you can divide each line into four equal parts using a scaling factor of 1/3. The two middle segments need to be adjusted to form the sides of an equilateral triangle.

29) What are Morphing and tweening?

Morphing refers to the process of changing the shape of an object to another form.

Tweening refers to a technique used in animation where objects that are defined by a series of points undergo a gradual transformation from one frame to the next. This gradual transformation results in a change in the shape of the object, which enhances the overall animation effect.

30) What are Peano curves?

A certain type of curve known as Peano curves can occupy all points in a plane, which gives them a dimension of two. These fractal curves are unique in their ability to fill the entire plane.

31) What is a Scripting system?

A scripting system is a tool that enables users to create specifications for objects and animation sequences using simple text input. By using these scripts, a range of objects and motions can be created and stored in a library for later use.

32) Define refresh/frame buffer.

The storage location for the definition of an image is referred to as the refresh buffer or frame buffer. It holds the array of intensity values for every pixel on the screen.

The frame buffer is a storage area that holds the data used to generate images in various types of video display monitors, such as CRTs, Raster Scans, Random Scans, LCDs, and LEDs.

33) What is the resolutions?

The term "resolutions" refers to the highest number of points that can be displayed without overlapping on a cathode ray tube.

Resolution refers to the amount of points that can be plotted horizontally and vertically per centimeter. The resolution is determined by counting the total number of points present in each direction.

34) Define Window and viewport.

A term used in computer graphics to describe a specific location on a display screen is known as a window. This location is determined by its world-coordinate area.

A "viewport" refers to a specific section of the display screen that is assigned to a given window.

35) Distinguish between window port and viewport?

A window port refers to a section of an image that will be displayed through a window.

A viewport refers to the method of displaying either a selected part or the design being viewed.

36) What are blobby objects?

There are certain objects that do not have a fixed shape and exhibit a malleable surface that changes in specific movements or when near other objects. These objects are commonly referred to as "blobby objects" due to their dynamic and fluid shape.

37) What are the Spline curves?

A spline is a strip that is bendable and is utilized for producing smooth curves between specific points. In computer graphics, the term spline curves refers to any curve that is made by combining several polynomial pieces that satisfy particular continuity conditions at the boundaries of those pieces.

38) What is the advantages of B spline over Bezier curve?

The order of the B-spline polynomial and the number of control points can be independently defined.

B-Spline is a mathematical technique that enables greater control over the shape of a curve or surface. It achieves this by allowing manipulation at the local level.

A Bezier curve is a type of mathematical function, typically cubic or quadratic, that outlines a path between two points using designated control points along the way. A Bezier spline is a group of multiple Bezier curves used in combination to create a complex curve or shape.

39) What is a Rasterizations?

Rasterization refers to the process of selecting the most suitable pixels for rendering images or graphic elements.

40) How will you represent a curve in graphics?

To generate a curve, there are two methods: one involves defining the mathematical tasks for the objects and the other requires user-specified points. In the former, a software package projects the equations onto the display plane and creates pixels based on the path of the projected plane.

41) Mention the uses of Chromaticity Diagram?

The chromaticity diagram is a tool utilized for determining the color of a gamut. Its main purpose is to determine if a color falls within the gamut line. If so, the color can be reproduced on the device or be described by the color system being used.

Interactive computer graphics are beneficial when they engage visitors and cater to their individual skills, interests, and goals. Similar to websites, no two visitors are exactly alike, so providing interaction is crucial. The user's involvement is a key feature of interactive computer graphics.

43) What does it mean by RGB?

The RGB color model is a type of color model that uses the additive system to create a vast array of colors through a combination of red, green, and blue lights. This model is commonly used in electronic systems such as televisions and computers but is also applicable in photography. Its name comes from the initials of the three primary colors used, R for red, G for green, and B for blue. The RGB model is primarily used for sensing, defining, and displaying images in digital systems.

44) What is VDU?

A visual display unit or monitor is an electronic device that shows images generated by a computer or other machines, without storing the data permanently. It comprises of a display device, an enclosure, and circuitry. In modern monitors, the display device is typically a TFT-LCD, whereas older ones use a CRT.

45) What is Projection?

The act of converting three-dimensional visuals into a two-dimensional display is referred to as a projection. This procedure involves transforming 3D objects into a 2D projection surface.

49) What is meant by Addressability?

Addressability refers to the resolution or density of dots per inch (d.p.i.) that can be created. Each dot's precise location is determined by its coordinates, such as (x, y), which then determines the position of the next dot in the succession, such as (x + y), (x + y + 1), and so on.

50) What is scan-code?

When a keyboard button is pressed, the keyboard auditor records a code that corresponds to the pressed key, known as a scan-code, and stores it in a section of memory called the keyboard buffer.
