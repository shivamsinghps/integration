11) How does a search engine work?

To gain an understanding of the inner workings of search engines, we can break down their tasks into three main stages: crawling, indexing, and retrieval.

Web crawling is a process carried out by software robots commonly referred to as web spiders or web crawlers. Every search engine utilizes its own web spiders to perform this task. The spiders scour websites and web pages they find, reading their content and following links to other parts of the website. This enables them to obtain information published on the World Wide Web. As soon as the crawler visits a page, it creates a copy of that page and adds its URL to the index.

The first step of a web spider's crawling process involves targeting frequently accessed servers and popular web pages. It then follows the links on these pages to discover new interconnected documents. The spider will also revisit previously crawled sites to check for updates or changes to the web pages. When changes are detected, the spider will make a copy of the updates to ensure the index is up-to-date.

Indexing refers to the process of creating an index of websites or web pages on the internet after they have been crawled. The indexing process involves carefully examining the web pages or websites crawled and then cataloguing them according to the quality and type of information they provide. This index of website information is stored in large storage centers. Essentially, the index serves as a sort of book containing a copy of every webpage that has been crawled by the spider. By collecting and organizing information from across the internet, it serves as a valuable resource for users seeking information online.

Retrieval is a crucial step in the functioning of search engines as it involves providing a list of relevant websites and information in response to the queries made by users. To achieve this, search engines rely on their databases to locate websites or web pages that contain the requested information. The relevant sites are then ranked based on the uniqueness and originality of their content, with the highest-ranked sites appearing at the top of the search engine results pages. Therefore, when users perform an online search, the search engine retrieves answers from its database, compiles a list of relevant sites, and presents them to users according to their degree of relevancy.

85) What is keyword density?

Keyword density refers to the ratio of how many times a particular keyword is used on a webpage compared to the total number of words on that page. For instance, if there are four occurrences of a keyword within an article consisting of 100 words, the keyword density will be 4%. This metric is often referred to as keyword frequency, as it measures how frequently the keyword appears on a page. While there is no magic formula for achieving optimal keyword density, a ratio of 2-4% is generally considered appropriate for SEO purposes.

101) What is CTR?

CTR is an acronym that stands for Click Through Rate. It measures the effectiveness of a search engine result page by determining how often users click on a particular link. The calculation involves dividing the number of clicks by the number of impressions, which is the number of times the link has appeared in search results. To illustrate, if a link has 100 impressions and 10 clicks, it has a CTR of 10%.

The success of a pay-per-click (PPC) campaign depends on its click-through rate (CTR). A higher CTR indicates a higher number of clicks on your ad, making it an essential metric to gauge the effectiveness of your campaigns. Therefore, it's crucial to maintain a high CTR to ensure successful PPC advertising.
